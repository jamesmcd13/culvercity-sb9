Building a Few Units in Culver City: The Multifamily Playbook

City of Culver City · R2 & the multifamily zones · seven ways to add units, compared — and which one to use · prepared by Conscious Communities Real Estate · July 2026

CHOOSE A PLAYBOOK: Single-Family · R1 (SB9 + ADU) Multifamily · R2 & up

Away from single-family land, the tools change. SB9 does not apply here — it only reaches R1. On an R2 or multifamily lot the extra units come from the base zoning plus a stack of state laws. Two levers decide almost everything: which zone the lot is (R2 < RLD < RMD < RHD), and whether it sits within a half-mile of an E-Line station — because transit-oriented housing quietly out-yields every other path, even on R1/R2.

The headline. For a “a few units” goal (3–8), there is one question to ask first: is the parcel within ½ mile of the Culver City or La Cienega/Jefferson E-Line station? If yes, Transit-Oriented Housing (CCMC 17.400.116) beats everything and needs zero affordable units up to 10. If no, the winners are buying an existing duplex–fourplex and stacking ADUs (fastest 5–9 rentals) or RLD base zoning + ADUs (best ground-up count).

1. Pick your path

1
Check transit first — it changes everything. If the parcel is within ½ mile of the Culver City or La Cienega/Jefferson E-Line station, use Transit-Oriented Housing (Path A). It supersedes base zoning, works even on R1/R2, and a project of ≤10 units owes zero affordable units. On a 7,500 sf lot that is 14–25 units by-right — for 3–8 units the roomy 2.5–4.0 FAR envelope makes a small building trivial.
2
Not near transit — decide by what you own and whether you'll rent or sell:
  • Own or can buy an existing duplex–fourplex, want rentals → ADU-on-existing-multifamily (Path E). An existing duplex → 5 units, triplex → 7, fourplex → 9 — all ministerial, FAR-free, zero parking. The fastest 3–8 with the least friction.
  • Vacant / redevelopable lot, want rentals, maximize count → RLD base zoning + ADUs (Path B). 5 / 7 / 9 by-right on 6k / 7.5k / 10k, +2 ADUs, no FAR cap, no minimum-density strings.
  • Simplest possible, R2 lot → R2 duplex + ADUs (Path D). 4 units new / 5 existing, no affordability, no subdivision, and a floor-area budget 33–44% larger than R1.
  • Want to sell the units individually → add SB1123 small-lot subdivision (Path C) for fee-simple/condo/TIC lots, or ADU condos (Path G) to sell ADUs off a rental building.
  • Need more units than base allows and can deed-restrict affordable → State Density Bonus (Path F) on top of an RLD/RMD or transit base.

Bottom line: near an E-Line station → Transit-Oriented Housing, full stop. Otherwise, buy-existing-and-add-ADUs for the fastest rentals, or RLD base + ADUs for a ground-up build; layer SB1123 or ADU condos on top only if the exit is for-sale.

3. The paths in detail

A. Transit-Oriented Housing — the winner near an E-Line station highest yield

CCMC 17.400.116 (Ord. 2026-007, implementing SB 79) · effective ~July 1, 2026

Any project of 5+ units within ½ mile of a designated stop may build to a transit density that supersedes the base zone — and it reaches into R1 and R2, which follow RMD standards to hit the allowed FAR. This is the biggest by-right upzone available, and a small 3–8-unit building fits its envelope easily.

Affordability is light: ≤10 units owe none; 11+ owe one of 7% ELI / 10% VLI / 13% LI. It stacks with the density bonus computed off the transit density, and parking minimums are zero (AB 2097, both stations qualify). Two stations: Culver City E-Line and La Cienega/Jefferson E-Line. There's even a two-phase move: build the base building, get certificates of occupancy so it's “existing,” then file for the full detached + conversion stack (confirm phasing with Building Safety).

D. R2 duplex by-right + ADUs — the simplest play, and roomier than R1

CCMC 17.210.020 Table 2-3 · no SB9 needed (R2 gets the duplex by-right)

R2 already allows a duplex by right — no SB9 — then ADUs stack: a new duplex + 2 detached ADUs = 4 units; an existing duplex adds a conversion ADU for 5. And R2's floor-area budget beats R1's flat 0.45 FAR at every size:

Lot sizeR2 budget (habitable only)R1 (0.45 FAR, incl. garage)R2 advantage
6,000 sf1,500 + 40% = 3,900 sf2,700 sf+44%
7,500 sf1,500 + 40% = 4,500 sf3,375 sf+33%
10,000 sf60% of lot = 6,000 sf4,500 sf+33%

Watch the tiers: the +1,500 sf base applies only below 8,000 sf; at 8,000 sf and up the budget is 60% of net lot area only (a 10,000 sf lot = 6,000 sf, not 7,500). R2 also excludes the garage, while R1's 0.45 must count an attached garage — so the real gap is bigger than the numbers show. The two detached ADUs are FAR-free on top.

C. SB1123 / SB684 small-lot subdivision — the for-sale exit

CCMC 15.10.945 (Ord. 2025-007)

This is an ownership tool, not a density adder: a ministerial (60-day, no hearing, no CEQA) way to carve up to 10 separately-sellable parcels — fee simple, condominium, co-op, land trust, or tenancy-in-common — at up to base density, plus one density-free ADU per lot. On the lot sizes here, base density binds first (a 7,500 sf RLD lot = 7 lots, not 10). Average primary home ≤1,750 net habitable sf; new parcels ≥600 sf (MF) / ≥1,200 sf (R1/R2). Use it when you want to sell small homes rather than hold rentals. For pure rentals, skip subdivision — base zoning + ADUs yields more.

F. State Density Bonus + local DOBI — more units for affordability

GC 65915 + CCMC Chapter 17.580 (Ord. 2025-002)

Deed-restrict affordable units and earn a density bonus plus concessions and unlimited waivers of development standards: roughly +20% to +50% of base (e.g., 15% very-low-income = +50%), stacking toward ~+100% under AB 1287, with Culver's local Tier and Community-Benefit bonuses layered on top. A 100%-affordable project near transit gets unlimited density. Off transit it's worth it only if the pro forma tolerates affordable units; near transit, apply it to the transit density for the highest count of all.

G. ADU condos — sell the ADUs individually

AB 1033 / CCMC 17.400.096 (newly allowed via Ord. 2026-004)

Adds no units — it lets you sell already-built ADUs as separate condominiums (a for-sale exit off a rental building). Requires a parcel map (≤4 units) or tentative + final map (5+), a pre-recording safety inspection, and HOA formation. Biggest catch: lienholder consent — your mortgage lender must sign off and can refuse, which may force a refinance or payoff. JADUs can't be sold separately.

5. Cross-path facts worth knowing

  • SB9 is R1-only. It does not reach R2 or the multifamily zones. R2's two units come from the base zone; the ministerial subdivision tool for R2 is SB1123, not SB9.
  • The ADU asymmetry is the crux. A proposed (ground-up) multifamily building gets only 2 detached ADUs; the same building, once existing, gets up to 8 detached (capped at unit count) + 25% conversion. That is why “buy existing” out-multiplies “build new.”
  • ADUs are always FAR-free and parking-free. As GC 66323 exemption units they stack on top of the base density and floor-area cap, with zero parking (including no replacement parking) — the cheapest units to add on any path.
  • Parking minimums are zero near transit. AB 2097 removes all parking minimums within ½ mile of a major transit stop; both E-Line stations qualify.
  • Fractional units round up (CCMC 17.120.010.C) — a special rule that turns 6.03 units into 7. It drives every RLD/RMD count.
